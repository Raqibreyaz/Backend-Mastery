# I think there are five internal operations

When I watch excellent engineers speak, they unconsciously perform these:

## 1. Locate

"What part of my knowledge graph is relevant?"

---

## 2. Anchor

"What is the simplest starting point?"

You naturally use names.

Someone else might use problems.

Another person starts with history.

---

## 3. Expand

Walk outward from the anchor.

Instead of randomly jumping.

---

## 4. Prune

Ignore 95% of what you know.

Beginners do the opposite.

They dump everything.

---

## 5. Linearize

Turn

```text
Graph
```

into

```text
Sentence 1

↓

Sentence 2

↓

Sentence 3
```

## A thought experiment for you

The next time you learn a topic, don't ask:

> "Can I explain this?"

Ask:

> **"If I had to produce exactly one opening sentence in under 3 seconds, what would it be?"**

That question forces your brain to solve the **Locate → Anchor → Linearize** problem before you're under interview pressure.

Over time, you'll stop "searching" for words and start recognizing familiar paths through your knowledge graph. That's what fluent technical speakers seem to do—they're not inventing explanations on the spot; they're traversing well-practiced routes through what they already know.

### Why this matters

The bottleneck in interviews is often not knowledge but **retrieval and serialization**. Improving that skill makes the knowledge you already possess accessible under pressure.

### Engineering superpower unlocked

The ability to transform complex, interconnected mental models into clear, sequential explanations that other engineers can immediately follow.

### Where this appears again

System design interviews, debugging sessions, design reviews, architecture proposals, mentoring, conference talks, and technical writing all depend on the same underlying serialization skill.
